May 17, 2016

Overview

  • Assess the effects of the merger Telmex-Comcel (now Claro).
    • Focus on effects on speeds and prices of hardwired internet.
  • Provide an explanation for the increase in speeds.
    • Supply side: economies of scope, cost sinergies.
    • Demand side: goods are complements.
  • The merger had competitive effects
    • Speeds increased, prices did not.
    • Complementarities are the best explanation.

Background

The merger that didn't happen

The merger on the news…


  • At the end of 2011 two telecom giants in Colombia seemed to cozy with one another.
  • In February 2012 they started releasing communications, reassuring the people and the regulators. In those communications they were adamant about their unwillingness to merge. After all, they said they were doing great separate.
  • A couple months later they were talking about operating separately after the same brand. By the end of the summer they were referring to the move as merger, but it was to late for regulators.

The merging parties

Comcel was the largest cellphone carrier (voice, SMS and two types of mobile Internet: cellphone and USB modem) at the time of the merger:

  • 93% of cities and towns
  • almost 70% market share
  • almost 70% of handsets sold

Telmex was the largest triple-play provider (land-line phone, Cable TV and Internet):

  • present in 24 of 33 states
  • average market share was 39%

The effects of the merger

First evidence of the effect of the merger

The effect of the merger: more details

  • In equilibrium the effects should extend beyond the merging firms.

  • I assess three types of effects:
    • Effect on the merging firms,
    • effect on their competitors, and
    • average effect on markets.

  • I compare the download speeds of:

    • the merging ISP and its largest competitors
    • merging firm's competitors and other similar firms.
    • markets affected by the merger and markets not affected by it.

The diff-in-diff design

I estimate the following:

\[ y_{fct} = \phi_0 + \phi_1 TELMEX_f + \phi_2 AFTER_t +\\ \phi_3 TELMEX_f \times AFTER_t +\\ \delta_{st} + \phi_f + \varepsilon_{fct} \]

\[ y_{fct} = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 FACES\_MERGER_f + \gamma_2 AFTER_t +\\ \gamma_3 FACES\_MERGER_f \times AFTER_t +\\ \delta_{st} + \gamma_f + \varepsilon_{fct} \]

\[ y_{ct} = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 AFTER_t + \alpha_2 MERGER_c +\\ \alpha_3 AFTER_t \times MERGER_c + \varepsilon_{ct} \]

Summary of the effects

  • The merged increased speeds (by at least 1.2 Mbps).
  • Rivals increased median speeds (.5 Mbps) but reduced their maxima.
  • Median speed in cities were the merger happened increased (.6 Mbps).

  • Results robust:

    • falsification test
    • by strata
    • propensity score

Why would speeds increase?

Two kinds of reasons

  • Supply side: the ISP reduces costs after the merger
    • access to pre-existing mobile networks
    • know-how from the mobile firm
    • acquisition of new assets
  • Demand side: the goods sold by the merging parties are complements.
    • Consumers demanding broadband are more likely to demand mobile services.
    • The merger internalizes profits.

Cost reductions are not it!

  • No good data to prove it. However,
    • increase in speeds is abrupt and widespread,
    • engineering craft, although related is not transferable, and
    • mobile networks are naturally congested (Calabrese (2013))

The theory of mergers of complements

There is a handful of papers examining the behavior of merging firms that, that prior the merger, produce complementary goods

  • Spector (2007)
  • Choi (2008)
  • Peitz (2008)
  • Anderson, Loertscher, and Schneider (2010)
  • Flores-Fillol and Moner-Colonques (2011)

Takeaway: Mergers of complements have potential for competitive effects

Utility and substitution patterns

Following Gentzkow (2007)

  • Suppose two goods A and B.
  • Defining \(\Gamma = (u'_{AB}-u'_B)-(u'_A-u'_0)\), the utility of consuming the individual goods and a bundle can be written as

\[u_A=\delta_A - \alpha p_A + \nu_A\] \[u_B=\delta_B - \alpha p_B + \nu_B\] \[u_{AB}=u_A + u_B + \Gamma\] \[\left[ \begin{matrix} \nu_A \\ \nu_B \\ \end{matrix} \right] \sim N\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & \sigma \\ \sigma & 0 \\ \end{matrix}\right)\]

Utility and substitution patterns

  • The choice probabilities for utility-maximizing consumers are given by

\[q_A = \int_u I(u_A \ge 0)I(u_A \ge u_B)I(u_A \ge u_{AB})dF(\mathbf{u})\] \[q_B =\int_u I(u_B \ge 0)I(u_B\ge u_A)I(u_B \ge u_{AB})dF(\mathbf{u})\] \[q_{AB} =\int_u I(u_{AB} \ge 0)I(u_{AB}\ge u_A)I(u_{AB} \ge u_B)dF(\mathbf{u})\]

  • Define \(Q_A = q_A +q_{AB}\) and \(Q_B = q_B + q_{AB}\)
  • Now the sign of \(\frac{\partial Q_A}{\partial p_B}\) determines the substitution pattern

Price of B increases and = 0


  • \(m\) substitutes \(AB\) for \(A\): \(\uparrow q_A = \downarrow q_{AB}\) and \(Q_A\) remains unchanged.
  • \(n\) has no impact on \(Q_A\)
  • Hence \(\Gamma = 0\) implies \(\frac{\partial Q_A}{\partial p_B}=0\) (goods are independent)

Price of B increases and > 0


  • \(m\) substitutes \(AB\) for \(A\): \(\uparrow q_A = \downarrow q_{AB}\)
  • \(o\) substitutes \(AB\) for outside option: \(\downarrow q_A\) and overall \(\downarrow Q_A\)
  • Hence \(\Gamma > 0\) implies \(\frac{\partial Q_A}{\partial p_B}<0\) (goods are complements)

Price of B increases and < 0


  • \(m\) substitutes \(AB\) for \(A\): \(\uparrow q_A = \downarrow q_{AB}\)
  • \(o\) substitutes \(B\) for \(A\): \(\uparrow q_A\) and overall \(\uparrow Q_A\)
  • Hence \(\Gamma < 0\) implies \(\frac{\partial Q_A}{\partial p_B}>0\) (goods are substitutes)

Empirical demand (I)

  • 4 individual goods \(h \in \{1,2,3,4\}\)
  • 16 possible bundles \(j \in \{0,1,...,15\}\)

  • Quarters are indexed by \(t\), cities by \(c\) and consumers by \(i=\{1,2...,N\}\)

  • The utility of consuming an individual good \(h\) is given by

\[\bar{u}_{ihct} = p_{hct} \bar{\alpha}_i + \sum_k x_{hk} \bar{\beta}_{ik} + \xi_{h}\]

\[\bar{\alpha}_i = \alpha + \sum_r z_{ir}\alpha_r^o + \nu_{ip} \alpha^u \] \[\bar{\beta}_{ik} = \beta_k + \sum_r z_{ir}\beta_{kr}^o + \nu_{ik} \beta_k^u \]

Empirical demand (II)

  • Second, the utility of a bundle j can be written as

\[u_{ijct} = \begin{cases} \varepsilon_{ijct}, & \text{if } j=0 \\[2ex] \sum_{h \in j} \bar{u}_{ihct}+ \Gamma_j +\varepsilon_{ijct}, & \text{if } j >0 \end{cases}\]

  • ijct is assumed to be distributed T1EV
  • j is zero for singleton bundles.

Empirical demand (III)

  • Integrating over the s the choice probability for bundle j, is

\[s_{jct} =\int_\nu \int_z \frac{\exp \left[\sum_{h \in j} u_{ihct}+ \Gamma_j \right]}{1 + \sum_{m=1}^{15} \left[\exp \sum_{h \in m} u_{ihct}+ \Gamma_m \right] }dF_\nu dF_z \]

  • From here I follow Berry(1994)
    • For given parameter values (logit estimates) simulate the shares
    • Contraction mapping to find mean utilities
    • Construct error term
    • Minimize the objective function

Final remarks

  • The merger had competitive effects: equilibrium download speeds increased more than prices.
  • There is evidence of strong complementary preferences for the goods offered by the merging firms.
